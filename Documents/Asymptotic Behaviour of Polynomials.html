General Notes

For all of the following, we can say:
  1. d is the highest power that n can be raised to.
  2. ad is stated to be non-zero, so we know that the growth of nd will not be rendered irrelevant by being multiplied by zero.
  3. Given the standard approach to evaluating run-times, we know that as we grow (particularly when tending towards infinity), the components of p(n) which are aini for i < d will not be significant - only the highest power of n drives the long-term growth trend in a meaningful way.
    Tending to infinity, ai-1ni-1 / aini tends to zero.
  4. Therefore, we are basically simplifying p(n) to nd for purposes of comparison with nk.
  5. Further to point 3 (etc.), we can say that the size of the highest power to which n is raised directly correlates to the expected order of growth over the long-term.
a. if k >= d, then p(n) = O(nk) By definition, big-O means that the expression p(n) will perform at worst in the order of nk.
Also by definition, big-O does not make any statement about whether or not p(n) may perform better than this.
Given the above general points, we can say this is equivalent to knowing that k must be at least equal to d (so it provides a loose statement of a reliable worst case) but may also be greater.
b. If k <= d, then p(n) = Ω(nk) By definition, big-Ω means that the expression p(n) will perform at best in the order of nk.
Also by definition, big-Ω does not make any statement about whether or not p(n) may perform worse than this.
Given the above general points, we can say this is equivalent to knowing that k must be at most equal to d (so it provides a loose statement of a reliable best case) but may also be less.
c. If k = d, then p(n) = Ө(nk) By definition, big-Ө means that the expression p(n) is tightly bounded by nk (it is within a constant factor of nk for both best and worst case) and cannot perform notably better or worse in terms of growth trend.
This is equivalent to saying that k must be equal to d, as if it were larger or smaller the order of growth would only be in O (allowing for meaningfully better performance than nk) or Ω (allowing worse performance than nk), not Ө.
d. If k > d, then p(n) = o(nk) Small-o notation requires that big-O be met, and then additionally states that nk is a weak upper bound - i.e. that the sequences diverge (specifically that the ratio nd / nk tends to zero).
This additional stipulation is equivalent to stating that nd cannot actually be equivalent to nk, i.e. k!= d.
So we are left with the fact that k must be greater than d, satisfying the tendency of the mentioned ratio to zero.
e. If k < d, then p(n) = ω(nk) Small-ω notation requires that big-Ω be met, and then additionally states that nk is a weak lower bound - i.e. that the sequences diverge (specificaly that the ratio nk / nd tends to zero).
This additional stipulation is equivalent to stating that nd cannot actually be equivalent to nk, i.e. k!= d.
So we are left with the fact that k must be less than d, satisfying the tendency of the mentioned ratio to zero.
